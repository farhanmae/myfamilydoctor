Pneumonia is a disease or infection that develops in one or both of the lungs. It is a contagious disease (can spread from person to person) that is caused as a result of several different reasons. Bacteria, virus and fungi can be the causal factor for pneumonia.

Overview

When the air sacs in the lungs become inflamed and fill with fluid or pus – it causes an infection and makes it very difficult to breathe. Both viral and bacterial pneumonia easily spreads throughout a community by inhaling airborne droplets from a cough or sneeze.

Symptoms

The symptoms of pneumonia vary in severity, ranging from mild to very serious. Shortness of breath (even while at rest), fatigue, sweating or chills, chest pain, fever, cough (that produces mucus), wheezing, loss of appetite, nausea and headache – are the most common symptoms of the disease.

Treatment

The treatment for pneumonia depends on your general health, the type of pneumonia and its severity. For most cases of bacterial pneumonia, oral antibiotics are used. In the case of viral pneumonia which will gradually clear on its own, at-home care is the best medicine. Sometimes, antiviral medication is prescribed. In individuals with other health issues, hospitalization may be needed. IV antibiotics, oxygen therapy and respiratory therapy are the various forms of treatment.

At-risk groups

Your age is a risk factor. An infant (up to 2 years of age) and a people above the age of 65 are at extreme risk for contracting the disease. Some people have a weakened immune system, i.e., these individuals are immunocompromised either as a result of disease or because of the use of medicines like steroids or cancer drugs. This group is extremely prone to catching pneumonia. Chronic medical patients suffering from asthma, cystic fibrosis, diabetes, or heart failure are also more susceptible as compared to the general population. Another at-risk group of people are those who’ve recently had a cold or the flu (in other words, a respiratory infection). People who are currently or have been recently admitted to a hospital are at risk fr=or pneumonia, especially if they have been or are on a ventilator. Stroke victims who have difficulty swallowing, smokers, people who consume excess alcohol, and people who have been exposed to lung irritants (example: pollution, fumes, certain chemicals, etc) are other groups of at-risk individuals.

Prevention

There are many things you can do to avoid contracting pneumonia. They are:
  • Vaccination
  • Quit smoking
  • Keep your hands clean
  • Cover when you sneeze or cough
  • Disposing of used tissues promptly
