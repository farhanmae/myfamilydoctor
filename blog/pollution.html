Awareness of Pollution control day by Physician in Kochi.

National Pollution Control Day

We are all aware of the adverse effects of pollution. Yet, we still keep polluting our environment without any concern for our future generations. On this day when we celebrate Pollution Control Day in our country, let us remind ourselves to act responsibly from this day forth. Let us take steps to curb pollution and also further educate ourselves about the risks of pollution. Read on to know more about the health hazards that result from pollution.

Effects on Human Health

The air we breathe, the water we drink, the food we eat – each thing that our life depends on is polluted these days. And as a result, our human body has become polluted, as well. We are in an ironic situation where we can never find purity in anything that is around us. And we ourselves are the reason behind this perplexing situation.

Health Hazards

Environmental pollution has an extremely negative impact on our health. It disturbs our balanced state of living by causing damage to our healthy body parts. Intake of these pollutants can cause very serious issues to young children, elderly people, and patients with respiratory or cardiovascular troubles. Given below is a list of health hazards caused due to pollution:

  • Respiratory disorders: Improper functioning of the lungs (Respiratory system).
  • Cardiovascular disinfections: Improper functioning of the heart (Cardiovascular system).
  • Neuropsychiatric complications: Improper functioning of the nerves (Nervous system).

Major Pollutants

Substances that cause pollution are known as pollutants. Pollutants can be of any form, size, and shape. They pave the way for all types of pollution that include air pollution, water pollution, soil pollution, etc. Here is a list of pollutants that play a major role in causing pollution.

Ozone: Ozone (O3) is a colourless gas that is mostly found in the troposphere (the upper region of the atmosphere). Ground-level ozone is formed as the end product of the reaction between VOCs and oxides of nitrogen gas that is eliminated by human activities or by other natural sources. Ozone increases the risk of Asthma and other respiratory disorders.

Carbon monoxide: Carbon monoxide (CO) is a colourless and odourless gas that is mainly produced as a result of inappropriate combustion of burning fossil fuel. This creates minor to severe poisoning. Carboxyhemoglobin (COHb) levels above 40% may be fatal.

Sulfur dioxide: Sulfur dioxide (SO2) is a colourless and highly reactive gas. It is an important pollutant which is the major cause of pollution. It is mostly eliminated from industries, fossil fuels, and natural volcanic activities. Consumption of this poisonous gas increases the risk of respiratory and cardiovascular diseases.

Nitrogen oxide: Nitrogen oxides are important pollutants that create air pollution. It irritates the lungs and increases the risk of respiratory infections. NO2 is the most poisonous of all nitrogen oxides. Consumption of NO2 at 2.0 to 5.0 adversely affects T-lymphocytes and other natural killer cells that defeat viruses.

Lead: Lead (Pb) is a toxic heavy metal. It is mostly eliminated from industries and vehicles. Pb settles in our body parts as it can not be readily excreted. Even low side if Pb can be fatal to the foetus and children. It adversely affects our liver, kidneys, nervous system, etc.

Final Thoughts

On this important day, let us all take a pledge and promise ourselves that we will, to the best of our abilities, help stop our environment from getting polluted any more. Act preemptively to curb all kinds of environmental pollution as there is no use in doing so after being affected by pollution-induced chronic illnesses, diseases and disorders. Let us take care of Mother Earth so that she can take care of us. With our newfound awareness, let us go ahead and take the first steps to save humanity from the effects of pollution. Let’s make hay while the sun shines.
