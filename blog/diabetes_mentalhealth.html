Diabetes and Mental health

Diabetes affects much more than blood sugar. A deviation in level of blood sugar can lead to sudden mood swings that can put emotional strain on relationships and personal life. For some people, there will be a stress of living with diabetes that can contribute to mood swings as well as concerns about possible complications. The physical effects lead to nervousness, anxiety, and confusion. Our friends, family and other closed ones can have trouble understanding these mood swings, but learning how diabetes affects mood and if we provide enough support, can help nurture stronger, healthier relationships. In this article, Dr.James Sebastian, best diabetes doctor of Kochi discuss how diabetes can affect a person's mood and relationships.

Diabetes and Mood Swings

Diabetes can affect a person's mood and lead to quick and serious changes. Symptoms of low blood sugar in our body can contribute to mood swings include:

  • Confusion
  • Hunger
  • Aggressiveness and irritability
  • Changes in personality or behavior
  • Difficulty in concentrating

Signs that a person may have high blood sugar include:
Difficulty in thinking clearly and quickly
Feel nervous
Feeling tired or lacking energy
Changes in blood sugar levels can affect a person's mood and state of mind. When blood sugar levels return to normal, these symptoms usually go away. Changes in blood sugar levels can cause rapid mood swings, including bad mood and irritability. This is especially true during episodes of hypoglycemia in which blood sugar drops below 70 milligrams per deciliter (mg / dL).However, when some people have hypoglycemia, they experience a slight feeling of euphoria

Anxiety, Depression & Diabetes distress

Diabetes can also lead to mental health problems that doctors call diabetes distress. This situation often ends with some elements of depression, anxiety, and stress.
The symptoms may not be a severe one for a doctor to diagnose diabetes as a mental illness, but can affect a person's quality of life. Causes of stress can include responsibility for managing the condition and worrying about possible complications.
Some people with diabetes may feel stressed and helpless as they try to manage their illness. Others may think that they are not coping well with their diabetes.
Others may always have an inbuilt fear whether their blood sugar is too high or too low, or need to worry about not detecting hypoglycemia quickly enough to avoid social embarrassment or danger if it occurs while sleeping or driving.
Some people get nervous when they worry about or what a friend will think of their diabetes or whether they will treat it differently. Family and friends can get involved too much and treat them like fragile people or try to organize their diet or exercise for them.
We need to make regular diet changes and control blood sugar levels as it can also increase stress levels and interrupt leisure and relaxation times, leading to increased feelings of anxiety and depression. The unpredictable elements can also cause anxiety and stress.

Some Lifestyle tips

  • Keep a routine meal schedule whenever it is possible
  • Exercise regularly
  • Enroll in a diabetes self-management program
  • Have a strong support network

When to see your diabetes doctor?

Friends and family should also help a person if they are showing signs of confusion and they may not know who or where they are. This could be a sign of low blood sugar or diabetic ketoacidosis, a serious complication of high blood sugar. People with diabetes may also benefit from having their current medication checked with their doctor to see if any of the prescription drugs could be contributing to diabetes-related conditions, mood swings, or unstable blood sugar control.
