Vitamin D

An Introduction to Vitamin D

Vitamin D is a fat-soluble vitamin which is both a nutrient you consume and a prohormone or precursor of a hormone required by your body. It is obtained through sun exposure, food, and dietary supplements. You need to get enough Vitamin D for your body to function well. It promotes strong bones and may even help prevent certain cancers. If you do not obtain enough Vitamin D, it may lead to symptoms such as muscle weakness, pain, fatigue and depression.

The Sunshine Vitamin

Also known as the “sunshine vitamin,” Vitamin D is produced in your body when sunlight strikes your skin. Melanin, i.e., the pigment in the skin acts as a shade and reduces the production of the vitamin. Therefore, people with darker skin tend to have lower blood levels of Vitamin D. Always ensure a sufficient level of Vitamin D is in your blood so that your body functions in the best way possible. Not having enough of the vitamin puts you at risk of developing bone abnormalities. For adults, the daily amount needed to maintain healthy bones and normal calcium metabolism is 600 IU.

The role of Vitamin D in the body

Vitamin D regulates the absorption and retention of calcium and phosphorous. Both these minerals are critical for building bones and teeth. Vitamin D is also needed by the intestines to stimulate and absorb calcium and by the kidneys to reclaim the calcium which would otherwise be excreted.

Vitamin D is needed for a healthy pregnancy. If you are deficient in Vitamin D, it puts you at greater risk of developing preeclampsia. You may also give birth prematurely.

It facilitates normal immune system function. It supports the health of our brain and nervous system. Vitamin D also supports lung function and health of the cardiovascular system. It provides improved resistance against certain diseases. Vitamin D regulates insulin levels, thereby supporting diabetes management.

Vitamin D has an important role in regulating our mood. It wards off depression & anxiety and boosts mood.

Adding Vitamin d supplements to our diet can help prevent heart disease and also help in losing weight. It can protect us against and possibly help to treat hypertension, diabetes, immune system disorders, certain types of cancer (colon, prostate and breast cancers), and multiple sclerosis.

Deficiency – causes and symptoms

Adsorption of sunlight is essential for our skin to produce vitamin D. But if you have darker skin and wear sunscreen, it reduces the body’s ability to absorb UVB rays (ultraviolet radiation B rays) from the sun. Sunscreen reduces our ability to synthesize it by 95%. Skin covered by clothing also inhibits the production of the vitamin. Spending more time indoors, living in big cities where the tall buildings block sunlight and being in an area with high pollution are other factors. Symptoms of Vitamin D deficiency include the following:

  • Regular infection / illness
  • Fatigue / tiredness
  • Back pain
  • Bone pain
  • Muscle weakness and pain
  • Stress fractures
  • Low mood / depression
  • Impaired wound healing
  • Hair loss
  • An overall sense of feeling unwell
If our bodies are deficient of Vitamin D for a long period, we may develop complications such as cardiovascular conditions, infections, neurological diseases, autoimmune problems, certain cancers (breast, prostrate, and colon) and pregnancy complications. Hence, it is vital that you receive enough of the vitamin through the foods you eat (egg yolk, shrimp, sardines, fortified orange juice, etc), nutritional supplements and exposure to sunlight.

How much Vitamin D do you need?

We need Vitamin D for the healthy functioning of the body. Normal blood serum levels of the vitamin range from 50 – 100 micrograms per decilitre. The recommended dose of vitamin D is expressed in international units (IUs) per day. IUs are the standard type of measurement for vitamins. For example, women who are pregnant or breastfeeding require 600 IU of the vitamin. The recommended IUs for vitamin D for different age groups are:

  • Infants – 400 IU
  • Children and teens – 600 IU
  • Adults up to age 70 – 600 IU
  • Adults over age 70 – 800 IU

Health risks of excessive Vitamin D

As important as it is, it is still possible to get too much of Vitamin D. Toxicity of the vitamin can cause non-specific symptoms such as anorexia, polyuria, weight loss, heart arrhythmias. It can even lead to calcification of tissue and blood vessels, which results in damage to the kidney and heart.
