Lipid Profile

Today the most common term we used to hear is the Lipid profile test. People are more curious and anxious about the outcomes of a lipid profile test. Here, Dr.James Sebastian, one of the best physicians in Kochi details you about the lipid profile test and what is its aftereffects .Read the blog. A lipid panel or you can simply say lipid profile is usually tested to get to know the details of lipid levels in your blood. So a common question arises? What is a lipid means? It’s nothing, but fats that can’t be dissolved in our blood. What does the lipid profile test result means? If the value in lipid profile test is found to be high, then the risk of getting heart diseases and strokes also increases. The lipid profile test also measures the total cholesterol level in the blood, i.e. both bad LDL cholesterol and good HDL cholesterol. This test can also measure triglycerides, a special type of fat found in the blood. Now let's talk about the importance of cholesterol in our body.

What is cholesterol?

Cholesterol is a soft material and on a regular basis it is very important for biological functions, such as an important role in the formation of cell walls, the formation of vitamin D by the body, the production of digestive bile in the small intestine and the production of bile it produces certain Hormones. If cholesterol levels rise rapidly, it is possible that it could become a silent threat, putting you at risk of several critical complications such as peripheral arterial disease, heart disease, and heart attacks. Cholesterol is bound to protein molecules called lipoproteins in the blood. The two main forms of cholesterol are: Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) & High-density lipoprotein (HDL). LDL is considered as the bad cholesterol as it causes several threats to our body by blocking our arteries which will end up in atherosclerosis. HDL is considered as the good cholesterol as it serves as a guard and eliminates all fatal and bad cholesterol. Let’s quickly go through some facts that cause high cholesterol in our body.

1. Stress

Acute stress in our life causes a number of health problems, including high cholesterol. Research has shown that it increases your risk of high LDL ("bad") cholesterol and lowers your HDL ("good") cholesterol. Because stress hormones such as cortisol and adrenaline trigger changes that can lead to increased blood sugar levels and inflammation

2. Medications

Some medicines can have unexpected effects on your cholesterol levels. It can be certain birth control pills, antivirals, anticonvulsants. Some medicines for high Blood pressure can also increase the cholesterol level in your body. Talk with your doctor, before taking such medicines.

3. Thyroid issues

Your body uses thyroid hormones to break down extra cholesterol that it doesn't need. So when you have an underactive or underactive thyroid, your total and LDL cholesterol levels go up. Talk to your doctor if you have symptoms of hypothyroidism such as fatigue, dry skin, muscle weakness, and pain.

4. Type 2 –Diabetes

Type 2 diabetes can cause high blood sugar. If there is too much sugar, it can stick to proteins such as cholesterol molecules. It makes cholesterol more harmful. For example, people with type 2 diabetes tend to have smaller, denser particles of LDL, which increases their risk of heart disease.

5. Menopause

Estrogen, a hormone, in women body, affects your cholesterol levels. When estrogen drops after menopause, your cholesterol levels rise. To make matters worse, post menopause women gain an average of 8-10 pounds. You will also stop exercising, which can increase your risk of high cholesterol

6. Sitting a lot

Whether at the desk or on the sofa, sitting for hours is harmful to your health. It has been linked to obesity, heart disease, and high cholesterol. If you sit too long, an enzyme that converts bad LDL cholesterol into good HDL cholesterol is reduced by 95%. To protect your heart, take frequent breaks. Get up at least every 30 minutes, and if you can, walk 5 minutes every hour.

7. Alcohol consumption

In addition to processing cholesterol, the liver also breaks down alcohol. So, drinking too much can affect your cholesterol levels. One study found that men who drank a lot (about 4½ glasses or more at a time) had lower cholesterol levels than those who didn't.

8. A high sugar consumption

Saturated fat is often considered to be the leading diet-related cause of heart disease. But too much sugar is to blame. A diet high in sweets causes your liver to produce more LDL cholesterol and triglycerides and less HDL cholesterol. One study found that people who got 10% or more of their calories from added sugar were up to three times more likely to have low HDL levels than those who ate less than half.

As prevention is better than cure, let’s discuss some methods to lower our cholesterol level in our body

  • Avoid excessive consumption of alcohol
  • Always try to maintain a moderate weight
  • Practice a healthy balanced diet
  • Exercise regularly at the way you like
  • Consume high variety of fiber rich foods
  • Drink plenty of water
  • Opt for baked, boiled foods rather than fried foods
  • Avoid fast, sugary pastry products when possible
  • Try to include omega 3 fatty acids in our diet
  • Avoid smoking
